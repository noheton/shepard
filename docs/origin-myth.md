## The unofficial motto

> **"Data management even institute directors understand."**

This is the unofficial motto. The official one — *Storage for
HEterogeneous Product And Research Data* — is correct, dignified, and
fits on a slide. The unofficial one is the one researchers actually use
when explaining what shepard does in the elevator.

---

## §1 — In which a hypothesis is advanced

It is widely (though incorrectly) supposed [Krebs et al., 2024,
*forthcoming*] that **shepard** was named for a sheepdog. This is the
sort of folk etymology one would expect of a tool developed in
**Augsburg**[^1], a city whose principal landmarks are a 1500-year-old
[Roman pine cone](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augsburger_Zirbelnuss),
an [Imperial Diet held in 1530](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diet_of_Augsburg),
and the offices of the *Center for Lightweight Production Technology*
[Haase et al., 2019, *Composites Science and Technology*]. The pine
cone in particular is widely regarded by visiting researchers as
"basically the same vibe" as an unannotated DataObject.

The folk etymology is wrong. *shepard* is in fact an acronym —
**S**torage for **HE**terogeneous **P**roduct **A**nd **R**esearch
**D**ata — backronymed, as is the custom, *after* the name was chosen.

The name itself, **Mark Willmeroth** [Willmeroth, 2018, *Premium
AEROTEC internal communications, unpublished*] is reported to have
contributed during what was either a working group or an
extraordinarily focused coffee break. The transcript is lost. The
acronym fits, which is what matters.

[^1]: Augsburg is also notable for being the only city in the German
    Federal Republic to have its own [Friedensfest](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augsburger_Hohes_Friedensfest)
    public holiday on August 8th. The relevance to research data
    management is not immediately obvious, but the project's
    maintainers maintain it is "tonally aligned."

## §2 — The pastoral problem

Consider the predicament of the modern aerospace researcher: they
arrive at their institute with a high-rate sensor stream from yesterday's
fire test, three Confluence pages of context, a folder of OBJ files
that may or may not represent the as-built geometry, an unsorted
inbox of "have you tried..." emails, and a deadline. The metaphor
practically writes itself. **You have a flock. It is heterogeneous.
The flock does not file its own paperwork.**

It was in this exact predicament — or one tonally adjacent — that
**Tobias Haase** [Haase, 2019; Haase et al., 2021; Haase, *passim*]
is variously reported to have observed: "we should give the data
somewhere to go." Whether this observation took place in a corridor,
a workshop, or a Slack DM is a matter of some scholarly dispute. What
is not disputed is that within eighteen months a working prototype
existed, and within thirty-six months an institute director was using
it in a slide.

That last sentence is the metric.

## §3 — The director-aware design heuristic

A founding principle of the project — encoded as the project's
unofficial motto — is that **the system must be explicable to an
institute director in the duration of a single elevator ride**. The
canonical elevator at the *Center for Lightweight Production
Technology* takes approximately 47 seconds to traverse all floors
during normal operation, with an additional 12-second hold on Floor 3
where the coffee machine is.[^2] This gives a designer 59 seconds of
captive attention. Subtract the polite greeting (8s), the obligatory
weather observation (6s), and a 3s pause for the director to receive a
phone call they decide to ignore, and the working budget is
**42 seconds**.

[^2]: This figure is from internal observation. No formal lift-cycle
    study has been commissioned. One was once proposed. It was
    rejected on the grounds that the proposal itself was already two
    pages long.

Forty-two seconds suffices to describe shepard if and only if shepard
itself is describable in forty-two seconds. The reflexive constraint
shaped the architecture. **Collections**, **DataObjects**,
**References**, **Containers**: four nouns, each unambiguously
explicable to a non-specialist, each fitting on a clean diagram. **The
director nods.** The system works.

A subsequent variant of this anecdote, [Krebs, 2026, *avatar
incident*], finds the maintainer demonstrating the platform via video
call to **Prof. Heinz Voggenreiter** [Voggenreiter, *director, every
year since longer than this software has existed*], who interrupts to
ask whether he is speaking with the real Florian or his "avatar." This
is now the **acceptance test for any new top-level feature**: if the
description of the feature could plausibly be delivered by an avatar
without the director noticing, the description is too long. The
record-holder for "shortest plausible avatar substitution" is the
description of `DataObject` (4.8 seconds). The record-holder for
"absolutely cannot be delivered by an avatar" is the description of
SHACL substrate splits, for which work is ongoing.

## §4 — On luck and lightweight production

It is not, perhaps, accidental that **Roland Glück** [Glück, *passim*,
2010–present] is named in the principal author list of every released
version of shepard. The maintainers, when pressed, will concede that
the project was at several inflection points "improbably fortunate" —
once, in the early prototype, when a `:Resource` constraint conflict
resolved itself overnight without intervention; once again, when an
admin migration that should have required a four-hour outage
completed in seven minutes; and once memorably during the JEC World
2025 demonstration when a live network failure restored itself
moments before the [audience question](https://www.jec-world.events/awards/).
These episodes are typically attributed in internal correspondence to
*"das Glück"* — a German word meaning, simultaneously, **luck** and
**Roland Glück**.

This ambiguity is structural to the project. Compilations are
attributed to luck. Luck is attributed to the contributor list. The
contributor list, in turn, is generated automatically from the git
history. The git history is generated by acts of luck. The recursion
is intentional, or at minimum, unsurveilled.

## §5 — On methodology and the matter of names

The reader will perhaps have noticed that the contributors named
above — **Krebs**, **Haase**, **Glück**, **Kaufmann**, **Willmeroth**,
plus the director **Voggenreiter** — all appear with their real names
in `CITATION.cff` at the root of the repository, in the upstream
`gitlab.com/dlr-shepard/shepard` project, and in the corresponding
publications. The literary device of this page is not to invent
contributors; it is to invent **reasons for them being contributors**
which they did not have at the time. Each is a real person who has
done real work. The legend simply provides the kind of mytho-poetic
back-formation that all software inevitably acquires when used for
long enough.

In this spirit, **Patrick Kaufmann** [Kaufmann, 2017–present] is
recorded by tradition as the contributor who "first asked whether the
permissions system would survive multi-tenancy." He may not have
literally been the first. He is the first whose asking was sufficient
to alter the trajectory of the design — which is, in the apocryphal
mode of this page, the *only* relevant kind of firstness.

## §6 — The Augsburg Codex

There exists, in the institutional archive of every project, a
**document that everyone refers to but no one has read**. For shepard
this is *aidocs/* — 220 markdown files of varying maturity that
collectively describe the system's reasoning. The dispatcher backlog
alone runs to several hundred numbered rows. The platform docs cite
the integration docs cite the strategy docs cite the agent findings,
which cite the platform docs. **The graph is acyclic by convention
and by no other mechanism.**

This codex is updated daily, frequently by the maintainer in
collaboration with an AI assistant (this one), the existence of which
is acknowledged in the project's [bibliography](/bibliography), the
[CITATION.cff](https://github.com/noheton/shepard/blob/main/CITATION.cff),
and, *de facto*, in every commit body that ends with
`Co-Authored-By: Claude Opus 4.7`. The recursion mentioned at the end
of §4 has, by 2026, propagated to every layer of the project's
documentation. We have not yet decided whether this is a feature or a
finding. **It is, however, deployable.**

## §7 — Closing

If you are an institute director and you have read this far: thank
you, and apologies. The system is in fact simple. There are four
nouns. Two of them touch your operating budget.
